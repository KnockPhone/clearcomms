Our method Why ClearComms rewrites the way it does

Every suggestion follows the same five principles, so the rewrite is consistent and you can explain any change to a colleague or client.

1. Plain English, reading age 9 to 11

Most people read more comfortably below their top reading level, especially on a phone. We aim low so the message lands first time. This mirrors long-standing plain-English guidance used across the NHS and GOV.UK.

2. Short sentences, everyday words

We split long sentences and swap officialese for plain words. This helps people with lower literacy, readers using English as an additional language, and anyone skim-reading.

3. People-first, non-stigmatising language

We name the person before the condition and avoid blame words. This draws on inclusive-language guidance from groups such as Mind, disability organisations, and the Samaritans media guidelines on suicide.

4. Accessible by default

CamelCase hashtags and meaningful link text so screen readers make sense of them, sentence case rather than block capitals, emoji used sparingly at the end, and a prompt to add image alt text. This aligns with WCAG and platform accessibility guidance.

5. Calm punctuation

We avoid shouting in capitals and we never use em dashes, choosing commas, brackets or a fresh sentence instead. Calmer punctuation reads better aloud and feels more trustworthy.

ClearComms is assistive. It speeds up the first draft of a review, but a person should always have the final say before publishing.
